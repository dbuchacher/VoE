# 364 Days — The Clean Year

The Enoch calendar uses 364 days. Not 365. Why?


## The Math

364 = 4 × 91 = 4 × 7 × 13 = 52 × 7

| Divides by | Result | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 4 | 91 | 4 seasons of exactly 91 days |
| 7 | 52 | 52 weeks, no remainder |
| 13 | 28 | 13 months of exactly 28 days |
| 28 | 13 | 28-day months = exactly 4 weeks each |
| 91 | 4 | quarter = 7 × 13 days |

365 = 5 × 73. Two primes. Divides by nothing useful. That's
why our calendar is a disaster.

364 is the largest number below 365.25 that divides cleanly by
both 4 and 7. Someone chose mathematical structure over
astronomical precision.

91 = triangular(13) = sum of 1 through 13 = 1+2+3+...+13.
364 = 4 × triangular(13). Four dimensions times the sum of 13.

28 is a perfect number: 1+2+4+7+14 = 28. Also close to the
lunar month (27.32 sidereal, 29.53 synodic). The month length
is a perfect number.

Intercalation: 365.2422 - 364 = 1.2422 days/year. In 6 years
that's ~7.45 days ≈ one week. Add one intercalary week every
6 years and drift is <0.5 days per 6-year cycle.

## The Day Length Answer

The year in SECONDS hasn't changed much — Earth's orbital
period is roughly constant. But the number of DAYS per year
decreases as days get longer (Earth's rotation slows from
tidal friction with the Moon, ~2.3ms per century).

A year had 364 days when a day was 24 hours 5 minutes —
roughly 13 million years ago. The "ideal" day length that
gives the clean calendar is barely longer than today's day.


## Why the Ancients Marked Equinoxes

and solstices — the orbital geometry crossing points:

  Stonehenge (3000 BCE) — summer solstice alignment
  Great Pyramid — cardinal direction alignment, equinox light
  Chichen Itza — equinox shadow serpent (March ~20, Sep ~22)
  Newgrange (3200 BCE) — winter solstice inner chamber light
  Angkor Wat — equinox sunrise over central tower

permanent infrastructure to make sure nobody forgot.
