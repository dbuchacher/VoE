The value of a good depends on the individual buyer's needs, not on the labor it took to produce.
Two people can look at the same object and assign it very different values.
A glass of water is worth little in a rainstorm but everything in a desert.
Marginal utility declines as you consume more of the same good.
The first slice of bread satisfies hunger, but the tenth adds almost nothing.
Prices carry information about scarcity and demand that no central planner can replicate.
When the price of a resource rises, it signals producers to supply more and consumers to use less.
Falling prices tell producers to redirect effort toward more valued goods.
The price system coordinates millions of decisions without anyone being in charge.
Spontaneous order arises when complex systems emerge from simple individual rules.
No one designed the English language, yet it has grammar, vocabulary, and coherent structure.
Markets are an example of spontaneous order where buyers and sellers coordinate through prices.
Incentives shape behavior more reliably than commands or intentions.
People respond to rewards and punishments, adjusting their actions accordingly.
A subsidy encourages more of an activity, while a tax discourages it.
Unintended consequences arise when incentives push behavior in directions the rule-maker did not foresee.
Both parties benefit from voluntary exchange because each values what they receive more than what they give.
Trade creates value even when one party is more efficient at producing everything.
Comparative advantage explains why specialization and trade make everyone better off.
The division of labor multiplies productivity by letting each person focus on what they do best.
Without prices, central planners cannot calculate which allocation of resources is most efficient.
The economic calculation problem shows that socialism lacks the feedback mechanism that markets provide.
Prices aggregate dispersed knowledge that no single mind could ever possess.
Hayek argued that the knowledge needed for economic decisions is scattered across millions of individuals.
Competition drives prices toward the cost of production over time.
Firms that produce more efficiently earn profits and grow, while inefficient ones shrink.
Monopolies restrict output and raise prices above the competitive level.
Barriers to entry protect incumbents and reduce the pressure to innovate.
Property rights give people the incentive to maintain and improve what they own.
Without secure property rights, investment and long-term planning collapse.
The tragedy of the commons occurs when no one owns a resource and everyone overuses it.
Assigning clear ownership converts a commons into a managed asset.
Externalities arise when a transaction imposes costs or benefits on third parties.
Pollution is a negative externality because the polluter does not bear the full cost.
Coase showed that clear property rights and low transaction costs can resolve externalities through bargaining.
Taxation transfers resources from private use to public use and always has a deadweight loss.
Deadweight loss represents value that is destroyed by the distortion of incentives.
Inflation erodes the purchasing power of money and distorts price signals.
Deflation discourages spending because buyers expect prices to fall further.
Sound money preserves its value over time and supports reliable economic calculation.
Savings channel current production into future investment.
Capital accumulation is the engine of long-run economic growth.
Interest rates coordinate the allocation of resources between present consumption and future production.
Artificially low interest rates encourage malinvestment in projects that cannot sustain themselves.
The business cycle alternates between booms fueled by credit expansion and busts that correct the errors.
Entrepreneurship drives innovation by discovering new ways to serve consumers.
Profit tells an entrepreneur that resources are being used to create more value than they cost.
Loss tells an entrepreneur to stop and redirect resources elsewhere.
Creative destruction replaces old industries with new ones that serve consumers better.
Opportunity cost is the value of the best alternative you gave up when you made a choice.
Every decision involves a tradeoff because resources are finite.
Sunk costs should not influence future decisions because they cannot be recovered.
Diminishing returns mean that adding more of one input eventually yields smaller increases in output.
Economies of scale reduce the average cost of production as output grows.
Diseconomies of scale set in when an organization becomes too large to manage effectively.
Public goods are nonrival and nonexcludable, which means markets tend to underprovide them.
Free riding occurs when individuals benefit from a public good without contributing to its cost.
Rent seeking diverts effort from creating value to capturing existing value through political means.
Regulatory capture happens when the regulated industry controls the regulator.
Moral hazard arises when someone takes greater risks because another party bears the consequences.
Adverse selection occurs when one side of a transaction knows more than the other.
Insurance markets struggle with adverse selection because high-risk buyers are most eager to insure.
Contracts reduce uncertainty by specifying obligations and remedies in advance.
Trust lowers transaction costs and makes complex exchanges possible.
Institutions are the rules of the game that shape how people interact.
Good institutions reward productive behavior and penalize predatory behavior.
Rules must align individual incentives with collective outcomes to produce social benefit.
Decentralized systems distribute decision-making and adapt faster to local conditions.
Centralized systems can coordinate large-scale action but suffer from information bottlenecks.
The knowledge problem limits what any central authority can know and control.
Feedback loops in markets correct errors automatically as prices adjust to new information.
Delayed feedback allows errors to compound before they become visible.
Resilient systems absorb shocks and recover without catastrophic failure.
Fragile systems appear stable until a small disturbance causes sudden collapse.
Redundancy is costly in the short run but essential for surviving unexpected events.
Diversification spreads risk across many assets so that no single failure is catastrophic.
