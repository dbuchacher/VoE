Voltage equals current multiplied by resistance, which is Ohm's fundamental law.
Current flows from higher voltage to lower voltage through any conductive path.
Resistance opposes the flow of current and converts electrical energy into heat.
A resistor in series adds its resistance directly to the total.
Resistors in parallel share current, and the combined resistance is less than the smallest one.
Power equals voltage times current in a purely resistive circuit.
Power dissipated by a resistor also equals the current squared times the resistance.
A capacitor stores energy in an electric field between two conductive plates.
The capacitor charges exponentially toward the supply voltage with time constant RC.
When the supply is removed, the capacitor discharges through the resistor with the same time constant.
After five time constants the capacitor is effectively fully charged or discharged.
Capacitors block direct current but allow alternating current to pass.
The reactance of a capacitor decreases as the frequency of the signal increases.
An inductor stores energy in a magnetic field created by current flowing through a coil.
Inductors resist changes in current and produce a voltage proportional to the rate of change.
The time constant of an RL circuit is the inductance divided by the resistance.
Together, an inductor and capacitor form an LC circuit that oscillates at a natural frequency.
The resonant frequency of an LC circuit equals one over two pi times the square root of LC.
At resonance, the impedance of a series RLC circuit drops to its minimum value.
A parallel RLC circuit reaches maximum impedance at its resonant frequency.
Quality factor Q measures how sharp the resonance peak is relative to its center frequency.
Higher Q means lower energy loss per oscillation cycle.
Bandwidth is inversely proportional to Q for a resonant circuit.
Higher bandwidth allows more information to pass through a channel per unit time.
A low-pass filter attenuates frequencies above its cutoff while passing lower ones.
A high-pass filter does the reverse, blocking low frequencies and passing high ones.
A band-pass filter allows only a narrow range of frequencies to pass through.
The cutoff frequency of an RC low-pass filter is one over two pi RC.
Decibels express the ratio of two power levels on a logarithmic scale.
A gain of three decibels corresponds to a doubling of power.
Operational amplifiers have very high gain and are used in almost every analog circuit.
An op-amp in negative feedback amplifies by a factor set by the external resistors.
A voltage follower has unity gain and is used to buffer signals without loading the source.
Kirchhoff's current law says the total current entering a node equals the total leaving it.
Kirchhoff's voltage law says the voltages around any closed loop sum to zero.
These two laws are sufficient to analyze any linear circuit.
Thevenin's theorem reduces any linear network to a single voltage source and series resistor.
Norton's theorem reduces the same network to a current source and parallel resistor.
Superposition lets you analyze each source independently and add the results.
A transformer transfers energy between circuits through a shared magnetic field.
The voltage ratio of a transformer equals the ratio of turns in its windings.
Transformers only work with alternating current because they require a changing magnetic field.
Diodes allow current to flow in one direction and block it in the other.
A forward-biased diode has a voltage drop of roughly 0.7 volts for silicon.
Zener diodes are designed to conduct in reverse at a precise breakdown voltage.
LEDs emit light when current flows through them and the semiconductor recombines carriers.
A transistor can act as a switch or as an amplifier depending on the circuit.
In saturation a bipolar transistor acts like a closed switch between collector and emitter.
In cutoff no current flows and the transistor acts like an open switch.
MOSFETs are voltage-controlled devices that draw almost no gate current.
CMOS logic uses complementary pairs of MOSFETs to minimize static power consumption.
Static power in CMOS is nearly zero because one transistor is always off in each pair.
Dynamic power increases with switching frequency and the square of the supply voltage.
Reducing the supply voltage is the most effective way to lower dynamic power.
Clock distribution networks must deliver the clock edge to every flip-flop simultaneously.
Clock skew occurs when different parts of the circuit receive the clock at slightly different times.
Signal integrity degrades as traces grow longer and frequencies grow higher.
Crosstalk happens when a signal on one trace induces noise on an adjacent trace.
Impedance matching prevents reflections at the boundary between different transmission lines.
A termination resistor absorbs the signal at the end of a line to prevent reflections.
Decoupling capacitors placed near power pins filter high-frequency noise from the supply.
Ground planes provide a low-impedance return path and reduce electromagnetic interference.
Differential signaling carries the signal as the voltage difference between two wires.
Differential pairs reject common-mode noise that affects both wires equally.
Analog-to-digital converters sample a continuous voltage and produce a digital code.
The Nyquist theorem says you must sample at twice the highest frequency to avoid aliasing.
Resolution determines the smallest voltage change the converter can distinguish.
A successive approximation ADC converges on the answer one bit at a time from the most significant.
Sigma-delta converters trade speed for very high resolution by oversampling and noise shaping.
Digital-to-analog converters reconstruct an analog voltage from a digital code.
Phase-locked loops lock an oscillator's frequency and phase to a reference signal.
PLLs are used for clock recovery, frequency synthesis, and modulation.
Switching regulators convert voltage levels more efficiently than linear regulators.
The duty cycle of a switching regulator determines the output voltage ratio.
Thermal management matters because every watt of loss becomes heat the package must dissipate.
Junction temperature must stay within limits or the device will degrade or fail.
Heat sinks increase the surface area available for convection and radiation.
Electromagnetic compatibility ensures a device neither emits nor is susceptible to interference.
