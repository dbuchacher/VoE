Quarks combine in groups of three to form protons and neutrons inside the atomic nucleus.
There are six flavors of quark: up, down, charm, strange, top, and bottom.
A proton contains two up quarks and one down quark held together by the strong force.
Gluons mediate the strong force that binds quarks together within hadrons.
The strong force grows stronger with distance, which is why isolated quarks are never observed.
Gravity is the weakest of the four fundamental forces but acts over the greatest distance.
Electromagnetism governs the interaction between charged particles and produces light.
The weak force is responsible for radioactive beta decay and neutrino interactions.
Every particle has a corresponding antiparticle with opposite charge and quantum numbers.
When a particle meets its antiparticle they annihilate and release energy as photons.
Positrons are the antimatter counterpart of electrons and carry positive charge.
Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another.
Momentum is conserved in every collision, whether elastic or inelastic.
Angular momentum is conserved when no external torque acts on the system.
Charge is conserved in every physical process without exception.
A particle exists in superposition until a measurement forces it into a definite state.
The act of measurement collapses the wave function to one of its eigenstates.
Entangled particles share correlated states regardless of the distance between them.
The uncertainty principle says you cannot know both position and momentum with arbitrary precision.
Planck's constant sets the scale at which quantum effects become significant.
The double slit experiment shows that individual particles create an interference pattern over time.
Light behaves as a wave when it diffracts and as a particle when it strikes a detector.
The photoelectric effect demonstrated that light carries energy in discrete quanta called photons.
Each photon has energy proportional to its frequency, following Planck's relation.
Time passes more slowly near massive objects, an effect predicted by general relativity.
A clock at sea level ticks slightly slower than an identical clock on a mountaintop.
GPS satellites must correct for relativistic time dilation to maintain accuracy.
Mass and energy are equivalent, related by the equation E equals mc squared.
Nothing can travel faster than light in a vacuum, which is the universal speed limit.
Special relativity shows that simultaneity depends on the observer's frame of reference.
The Higgs field gives mass to elementary particles through their interaction with it.
Neutrinos are nearly massless particles that rarely interact with ordinary matter.
Dark matter accounts for roughly a quarter of the total energy content of the universe.
Dark energy drives the accelerating expansion of the universe and remains poorly understood.
The standard model describes all known fundamental particles and three of the four forces.
Gravity is not included in the standard model and resists quantization.
String theory proposes that particles are vibrations of tiny one-dimensional strings.
Thermodynamics governs the flow of heat and the concept of entropy.
Entropy measures the number of microscopic arrangements consistent with a macroscopic state.
The second law of thermodynamics says entropy in a closed system never decreases.
Heat flows spontaneously from hot objects to cold ones, never the reverse.
Absolute zero is the lowest possible temperature where all thermal motion ceases.
Superconductors carry electric current with zero resistance below a critical temperature.
Superfluids flow without viscosity and can climb the walls of their container.
Bose-Einstein condensates form when particles are cooled to near absolute zero and behave as one.
Fermions obey the Pauli exclusion principle and cannot occupy the same quantum state.
Bosons can occupy the same state, which is why lasers produce coherent light.
The electromagnetic spectrum spans radio waves through gamma rays with visible light in between.
Higher frequency radiation carries more energy per photon.
X-rays penetrate soft tissue because their wavelength is shorter than atomic spacing.
Blackbody radiation follows a spectrum that depends only on temperature.
The cosmic microwave background is the remnant radiation from the early universe.
Redshift tells us that distant galaxies are moving away and the universe is expanding.
Nuclear fusion powers stars by combining light nuclei into heavier ones and releasing energy.
Fission splits heavy nuclei and releases energy exploited in reactors and weapons.
The half-life of a radioactive isotope is the time for half the atoms to decay.
Tunneling allows particles to pass through barriers that classical physics forbids.
Quantum tunneling is essential for nuclear fusion in stars and for semiconductor devices.
Virtual particles appear briefly from the vacuum and mediate fundamental forces.
The Casimir effect demonstrates that vacuum fluctuations exert measurable forces between plates.
Symmetry breaking explains how the early universe transitioned from a uniform state to diverse particles.
Noether's theorem connects every continuous symmetry to a conserved quantity.
Translational symmetry in time leads to conservation of energy.
Translational symmetry in space leads to conservation of momentum.
Rotational symmetry leads to conservation of angular momentum.
Gauge symmetry underlies the mathematical structure of all fundamental interactions.
Phase transitions occur when a system changes state, such as water freezing into ice.
Critical phenomena near phase transitions exhibit universal scaling behavior.
Chaos theory shows that deterministic systems can be practically unpredictable.
Sensitive dependence on initial conditions means tiny differences grow exponentially over time.
The arrow of time points from low entropy past to high entropy future.
Information is physical and erasing a bit costs a minimum amount of energy.
Landauer's principle connects thermodynamics directly to computation.
Wheeler proposed that every physical quantity derives from information, summarized as it from bit.
The holographic principle suggests that a volume of space can be encoded on its boundary.
Quantum computing exploits superposition and entanglement to solve certain problems exponentially faster.
A qubit can be zero, one, or any coherent combination of both.
Decoherence destroys quantum information as a system interacts with its environment.
