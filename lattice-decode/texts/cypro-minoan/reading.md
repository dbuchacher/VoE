# Cypro-Minoan: The Script in the Middle

## The Bridge Between Two Worlds

There is a script sitting between two others that we can already read, and for
that reason it may be the most decipherable undeciphered writing system on
earth.

The script is Cypro-Minoan. It was used on the island of Cyprus from roughly
1550 to 1050 BCE, during the late Bronze Age, when Cyprus was a copper-trading
hub at the crossroads of the Aegean, the Levant, and Egypt. The people who
used it left behind about 250 inscriptions — clay tablets, cylinder seals,
clay balls, stone objects — and then the Bronze Age collapsed, and the script
went silent.

What makes Cypro-Minoan extraordinary is its position in history. It did not
arise from nowhere and vanish without issue. It is the middle term in a
connected chain of writing systems, and we can read both ends of that chain.

The chain works like this. On Crete, around 1800 BCE, a civilization we call
Minoan used a script called Linear A. We cannot read it. Around 1450 BCE, the
Mycenaean Greeks adapted Linear A into a new script, Linear B, which encodes
early Greek. Michael Ventris cracked Linear B in 1952. That gives us a
partial key to Linear A — not enough to read it, but enough to identify which
Linear A signs probably carry which phonetic values.

Now the other branch. Around 1550 BCE, someone in Cyprus took Linear A and
adapted it for use on the island. This became Cypro-Minoan. Then, around 800
BCE, the Cypro-Minoan script evolved into the Classical Cypriot syllabary,
which encodes both Greek and a mysterious non-Greek language called
Eteocypriot. The Cypriot syllabary was deciphered in the nineteenth century.
We can read it completely.

So Cypro-Minoan has a partially-readable ancestor on the left — Linear A —
and a fully-readable descendant on the right — the Cypriot syllabary. This
is a situation that does not exist for any other undeciphered script. It means
that instead of attacking the problem from one direction, we can close in from
both sides at once.


## What the Script Looks Like

Cypro-Minoan is a syllabary. Each sign represents a syllable — typically a
consonant followed by a vowel, like ka, ta, na, or pa. The descendant script,
the Cypriot syllabary, has 55 signs arranged in a clean grid: thirteen
consonant series (k, l, m, n, p, r, s, t, w, and a few others), each paired
with five vowels (a, e, i, o, u), plus five pure vowel signs.

Cypro-Minoan has somewhere between 74 and 88 signs in regular use, depending
on how you count variants. That is a bit more than the Cypriot syllabary, but
the same order of magnitude. The simplest interpretation is that Cypro-Minoan
was running the same type of system — consonant plus vowel — and that the
later Cypriot syllabary inherited that structure and simplified it somewhat
over the centuries.

The corpus is small. About four thousand sign instances survive in legible
condition. For comparison, Linear B had roughly thirty thousand sign instances
when Ventris worked on it, and that was considered thin. Cypro-Minoan is
thinner. But what it lacks in volume it partially compensates for in
structural constraint: we know where it came from and where it went.


## The Anchor Signs

About fifteen to nineteen signs in Cypro-Minoan have what scholars call
"reasonably secure" phonetic values. These are signs where the evidence
converges from multiple independent directions.

The convergence works like this. Paleographers can trace the visual shape of
a sign backward to Linear A and forward to the Cypriot syllabary. Where a
Cypro-Minoan sign has a clear visual ancestor in Linear A and a clear visual
descendant in the Cypriot syllabary, and where those two endpoints carry the
same phonetic value, the middle sign probably carries that value too.

The most secure identifications, where multiple researchers independently
arrived at the same conclusion, are a set of seven signs: the signs for ta,
ka, pa, sa, na, a (the vowel), and ro or lo. A second tier of nine additional
signs — including ma, te, ti, ni, se, si, ke, re, and lo — have good evidence
from one direction but are less fully confirmed. That gives us roughly sixteen
anchor signs out of a total inventory of about eighty-five.

Sixteen out of eighty-five is not enough to read texts. But it is enough to
anchor a grid. The anchor signs tell us where the consonant rows and vowel
columns should be. Once the grid structure is established, the remaining
signs can be slotted in by their distributional behavior — how often they
appear, where in a word they tend to fall, and what other signs they appear
next to.


## One Script, Not Three

For most of the twentieth century, scholars divided Cypro-Minoan into three
separate sub-systems. CM1 was the main body of inscriptions — clay balls,
seals, and various objects from across Cyprus. CM2 was a small group of clay
tablet fragments from the site of Enkomi, dated to the twelfth century BCE,
which look angular and cuneiform-like. CM3 was a group of inscriptions found
at Ugarit, on the Syrian coast, presumably left by Cypriot merchants.

The division seemed sensible. CM2 looks visually different from CM1, and CM3
comes from a different country entirely.

But recent work has overturned this picture. A 2022 deep-learning study
published in PLOS ONE trained a model to classify the signs, and the model
found that CM1 and CM2 are the same script. The visual differences between
them are not linguistic differences. They are medium differences. CM2 looks
angular because it is written on clay with a stylus, the same way that
cuneiform evolved from rounded pictographs into angular wedges not because
the language changed but because clay rewards angular marks. The script did
not fork. The scribes just switched materials.

The scholar Silvia Ferrara, who compiled the most comprehensive Cypro-Minoan
signary, reached the same conclusion independently. CM1, CM2, and CM3 coexisted
simultaneously across the Late Bronze Age. They are not three stages of
development. They are one script used in different places on different surfaces.

This matters enormously for analysis. If CM1 and CM2 are the same script, then
their combined corpus — roughly three thousand syllabic sign instances — can
be analyzed as a single body of evidence. More data means more statistical
power. More statistical power means the distributional analysis is more
reliable.


## What the Enkomi Tablets Probably Say

The longest continuous Cypro-Minoan text is not a literary composition or a
dedication to a god. It is almost certainly a list of things.

The Enkomi cylinder (catalogued as ENKO Arou 001) contains 217 signs — the
single longest Cypro-Minoan inscription known. For decades, marks on the
cylinder that were interpreted as word dividers were reanalyzed in a 2020
study in the journal Kadmos, which argued that one set of those marks is
actually numerals. If that reading is right, the cylinder is an accounting
document: a record of quantities, perhaps of copper ingots, grain, oil, or
luxury goods.

This would not be surprising. Nearly all the long Bronze Age administrative
texts we can read are accounts. Linear A is full of them. Linear B is almost
nothing else. Clay tablets are expensive to make but cheap to smash when you
are done with the accounting season — and so they survived in enormous numbers
as debris from administrative fires. The Enkomi tablets, the four fragmentary
clay texts that make up the CM2 group, probably record the same kind of
palace-economy bookkeeping that fills the archives of Knossos and Pylos.

The Kadmos study also noted that sign-groups on the Enkomi cylinder tend to
end in a sequence that transliterates as -o-ti. That small observation turns
out to have large implications.


## A Thousand Years of Continuity

After the Bronze Age collapse, around 1200 BCE, Cypro-Minoan went out of use.
But about four centuries later, Cyprus developed the Classical Cypriot
syllabary, which we can read. Among the texts written in that later script is
a body of inscriptions in a language that is not Greek. Scholars call it
Eteocypriot — "true Cypriot" or "original Cypriot." It is not related to any
language we can identify with certainty, and it remains untranslated at the
level of meaning. But we know what the words sound like, because the script
that encodes them is fully deciphered.

Eteocypriot texts, dated from the seventh to the fourth century BCE, frequently
end in the sequence -o-ti. The same sequence that the Kadmos study identified
as a recurring ending on the Enkomi cylinder.

If that match is real — and it is contested, but the argument is serious — then
we are looking at a single language, or at minimum a single grammatical
morpheme, persisting across more than a thousand years of Cypriot history.
The people writing Eteocypriot on Cyprus in 500 BCE may have been the
linguistic descendants, in an unbroken line, of the people writing
Cypro-Minoan in 1400 BCE.

This would mean that the language of Cypro-Minoan did not disappear. It
survived the Bronze Age collapse, lost its script for a few centuries, and
then when Cyprus developed a new writing system, the old language was still
there to be written down. The script changed. The language endured.

And if the Eteocypriot connection holds, then every Eteocypriot text becomes
a partial key to Cypro-Minoan. We know how Eteocypriot words sound. We do not
know what they mean, but we know their phonetic structure, their endings, the
patterns that mark them as grammatical forms. That structural knowledge flows
backward through time toward the Bronze Age inscriptions.


## What Would Crack It

The pieces are almost in place. What is missing is a machine-readable corpus.

In 2021, Unicode assigned code points to Cypro-Minoan (U+12F90 through
U+12FF2, ninety-nine code points). This means that in principle, someone with
access to the scholarly transcriptions could type out every Cypro-Minoan
inscription in machine-readable form. Once that exists, the analysis becomes
straightforward computation.

The computation would work as follows. For each sign in the corpus, measure
four properties: how often does it appear (frequency); where in words does it
tend to fall (positional preference); how long are the words it appears in
(word length tendency); and how many different neighboring signs does it
combine with (combinatorial range). These four measurements place each sign
at a location in a four-dimensional space.

The same measurements applied to the Cypriot syllabary — which we can already
read — give us a map of where ta sits in that space, where ka sits, where na
sits, and so on for all fifty-five signs. The same measurements applied to
Linear A give us a corresponding map for the ancestor script.

The Cypro-Minoan signs then get placed in the same space. For each
Cypro-Minoan sign, find its nearest neighbor in the Linear A map. Find its
nearest neighbor in the Cypriot syllabary map. If both neighbors point to the
same phonetic value — if the Cypro-Minoan sign sits close to Linear A's ta in
the ancestor map and also close to the Cypriot syllabary's ta in the
descendant map — then that Cypro-Minoan sign almost certainly represents ta.

The bridge constraint, working from both ends, narrows the problem. Not every
sign will resolve cleanly. Some Cypro-Minoan signs probably have no surviving
relative in either direction — values that merged or split during the
seven-hundred-year journey from Linear A to the Cypriot syllabary. But the
anchor signs establish the grid, the distributional analysis fills it in, and
the Cypriot syllabary provides the target we are aiming for.

The estimate, based on how well this method performed when applied to the
relationship between Linear A and Linear B, is that roughly forty of the
eighty-five Cypro-Minoan signs should be identifiable with high confidence
once the distributional data is available. Another twenty or so should be
constrainable to two or three candidate values. That is not a complete
decipherment, but it is enough to read the content of many inscriptions.

The scholarly transcriptions exist. The Unicode encoding exists. The
analytical framework is ready to run. The Enkomi tablets are sitting in the
Cyprus Museum in Nicosia. The longest inscription, the cylinder with its
217 signs, its numerals, its -o-ti endings, its echo of a language that
survived a civilization's collapse — it is waiting to be read.

Cypro-Minoan is the best candidate among all undeciphered scripts for a
complete structural analysis. Not because the problem is easy — the corpus
is thin, the language behind it remains unknown, and the connection to
Eteocypriot is not proven. But the architecture of the problem is right.
The ancestor is partly legible. The descendant is fully legible. The method
is validated. The data just needs to be put into the machine.
